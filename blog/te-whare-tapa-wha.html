Dev Academy has a commitment to Te Tiriti O Waitangi and is working out how to honour and integrate its principles. We all live in Aotearoa and drawing on a wellbeing model grounded in Te Ao Māori makes sense. We teach the whole person at Dev Academy, and this holistic approach is aligned with that.

What is Te Whare Tapa Whā?

Te Whare Tapa Whā is a model of wellbeing built around four walls of a wharenui (meeting house) — taha tinana (physical), taha hinengaro (mental & emotional), taha wairua (spiritual), and taha whānau (family & social). All four are supported by whenua (the land, our foundations). If one wall is weak, the whole house is affected — same as our wellbeing.

Taha Tinana — Physical Wellbeing

  • Go to the gym or do home workouts 3x or more a week
  • Eat decent kai (less energy drinks, more real food)
  • Keep a proper sleep routine (aim for 7-8 hours)

Taha Hinengaro — Mental & Emotional Wellbeing

  • Daily journaling or blog reflection to clear my head
  • Use mindfulness or breathing exercises when stressed
  • Balance screen time with breaks outdoors

Taha Wairua — Spiritual Wellbeing

  • Take a few minutes each morning to set intentions / gratitude
  • Listen to music/audiobooks that grounds me
  • Spend time reflecting on values and purpose, not just tasks

Taha Whānau — Family & Social Wellbeing

  • Dinner with my whānau
  • Quality time with my kids (take them out, playing games)
  • Keep in touch with mates so I'm not isolating while studying

Whenua — Land & Environment

  • Go for walks outside, even short ones during breaks
  • Touch base with nature — barefoot on the grass, trips to the beach
  • Remember my connection to Aotearoa and where I come from

Final Thoughts

This model is a good reminder that studying isn't just about grinding code — I need to look after my whole self. If one wall is off, it affects the rest. By keeping these practices in balance, I'll have a stronger foundation for learning and for life.
